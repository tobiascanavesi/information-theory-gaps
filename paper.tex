\begin{abstract}
We present the first systematic information-theoretic analysis of the prime gap sequence $g_n = p_{n+1} - p_n$.
Using Shannon block entropy, mutual information, Lempel--Ziv complexity, and distributional goodness-of-fit tests, we measure the information content, dependence structure, compressibility, and convergence to the Gallagher--Poisson model across five scales from~$10^4$ to~$10^8$ with $2 \times 10^5$ primes per scale.
Our main findings are:
(1)~the entropy rate $h_k = H_k - H_{k-1}$ converges rapidly, revealing that consecutive gaps carry far less information than an i.i.d.\ model predicts (redundancy $\approx 23$--$27$ bits at block size $k = 10$);
(2)~mutual information at lag~$1$ is $\approx 0.33$ bits---orders of magnitude larger than what the linear autocorrelation ($r \approx -0.04$) would suggest---indicating substantial \emph{nonlinear} dependence, and remains detectable at all lags tested ($\ell \le 100$);
(3)~the Lempel--Ziv complexity of the gap sequence is $\approx 7\%$ below that of shuffled surrogates, with the ratio approaching~$1$ at larger scales;
(4)~distributional tests against Exp$(1)$ reject at all scales but with monotonically decreasing test statistics, and the variance-to-mean ratio of prime counts in Poisson windows increases toward~$1$, quantifying the rate of convergence to the Gallagher prediction;
(5)~primes~$\equiv 5 \pmod{6}$ exhibit slightly higher gap entropy than primes~$\equiv 1 \pmod{6}$, connecting to the Hardy--Littlewood singular series.
All code and data are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ====================================================================

The prime gap sequence $g_n = p_{n+1} - p_n$ is one of the most studied objects in analytic number theory. Classical results characterize its average behavior (the prime number theorem gives $\langle g_n \rangle \sim \log p_n$), its extreme values (the Cram\'er conjecture~\cite{Cramer1936}, the Maynard--Tao theorem on bounded gaps~\cite{Maynard2015}), and its distributional properties (the Gallagher conjecture~\cite{Gallagher1976} predicting local Poisson statistics conditional on the Hardy--Littlewood $k$-tuple conjecture).

Despite this extensive literature, the \emph{information-theoretic} properties of the gap sequence have received almost no systematic attention. Shannon entropy, mutual information, and algorithmic complexity provide a complementary lens that captures aspects of the sequence's structure---including nonlinear dependencies and compressibility---that traditional statistical measures like autocorrelation cannot detect.

In this paper, we present the first comprehensive information-theoretic characterization of $(g_n)$, organized into five experiments:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Block entropy and entropy rate} (\S\ref{sec:block-entropy}): We measure the Shannon entropy $H_k$ of overlapping $k$-tuples of consecutive gaps and estimate the entropy rate $h = \lim_{k \to \infty} h_k$, where $h_k = H_k - H_{k-1}$.

\item \textbf{Mutual information} (\S\ref{sec:mi}): We compute $\MI(g_n; g_{n+\ell})$ for lags $\ell = 1, \ldots, 100$ and compare to the linear autocorrelation.

\item \textbf{Lempel--Ziv complexity} (\S\ref{sec:lz}): We measure the LZ76 factorization complexity of $(g_n)$ and compare to shuffled and synthetic Poisson surrogates.

\item \textbf{Goodness-of-fit to the Gallagher--Poisson model} (\S\ref{sec:gof}): We test the normalized gaps $g_n / \langle g_n \rangle$ against the exponential distribution and counts in Poisson windows against the Poisson distribution.

\item \textbf{Conditional entropy by residue class} (\S\ref{sec:conditional}): We measure how entropy varies when primes are partitioned by their residue modulo~$6$.
\end{enumerate}

All measurements are performed at five scales (primes near $10^4, 10^5, 10^6, 10^7, 10^8$) with $N = 2 \times 10^5$ primes per scale, enabling the study of scale-dependent behavior.

\section{Definitions and Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ====================================================================

Let $p_1 = 2 < p_2 = 3 < p_3 = 5 < \cdots$ be the primes. Fix a starting scale $x$ and let $p_1(x) < p_2(x) < \cdots < p_N(x)$ be $N$ consecutive primes starting near $x$. Define the gap sequence $g_i = p_{i+1}(x) - p_i(x)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, N-1$.

\begin{definition}[Block entropy]
For $k \ge 1$, define the $k$-block entropy
\[
H_k = - \sum_{\mathbf{w} \in \mathcal{A}^k} \hat{p}(\mathbf{w}) \log_2 \hat{p}(\mathbf{w}),
\]
where $\hat{p}(\mathbf{w})$ is the empirical frequency of the $k$-tuple $\mathbf{w} = (w_1, \ldots, w_k)$ among the overlapping windows $(g_i, g_{i+1}, \ldots, g_{i+k-1})$, and $\mathcal{A} = \{2, 4, 6, 8, \ldots\}$ is the alphabet of even positive integers.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Entropy rate]
The $k$-th entropy rate estimate is $h_k = H_k - H_{k-1}$ for $k \ge 2$, with $h_1 = H_1$. For a stationary ergodic process, $h_k \to h$ as $k \to \infty$, where $h$ is the entropy rate of the process.
\end{definition}

For an i.i.d.\ process, $H_k = k \cdot H_1$ and $h_k = H_1$ for all $k$. The \emph{redundancy} $R_k = k \cdot H_1 - H_k \ge 0$ measures the total dependence captured up to block size $k$.

\begin{definition}[Mutual information]
For random variables $X, Y$ with discrete joint distribution,
\[
\MI(X; Y) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X, Y).
\]
We apply the Miller--Madow bias correction~\cite{MillerMadow}: subtract $(|\mathcal{A}_X| - 1)(|\mathcal{A}_Y| - 1) / (2 n \ln 2)$ from the raw estimate.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Lempel--Ziv complexity]
The LZ76 complexity $C(s)$ of a finite sequence $s = s_1 s_2 \cdots s_n$ is the number of distinct phrases in the sequential parsing of $s$ into shortest substrings not previously encountered~\cite{LZ76}. For a random i.i.d.\ sequence over an alphabet of size $|\mathcal{A}|$, $C(s) \sim n / \log_{|\mathcal{A}|} n$ almost surely.
\end{definition}

\section{Block Entropy and Entropy Rate}
\label{sec:block-entropy}
% ====================================================================

Table~\ref{tab:block-entropy} summarizes the block entropy measurements at the largest scale ($10^8$). The key observation is the rapid decay of the entropy rate $h_k$: by $k = 6$, the marginal information contributed by an additional gap is less than $0.4$ bits, and by $k = 10$ it is essentially zero.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Block entropy at scale $10^8$ ($N = 200{,}000$ primes).}
\label{tab:block-entropy}
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
\toprule
$k$ & $H_k$ (bits) & $h_k$ (bits) & $H_k^{\mathrm{iid}}$ (bits) & Redundancy \\
\midrule
1  & 4.481 & 4.481 & 4.481  & 0.000 \\
2  & 8.634 & 4.153 & 8.963  & 0.329 \\
3  & 12.499 & 3.865 & 13.444 & 0.946 \\
4  & 15.607 & 3.109 & 17.926 & 2.318 \\
5  & 17.186 & 1.579 & 22.407 & 5.221 \\
6  & 17.557 & 0.370 & 26.889 & 9.332 \\
7  & 17.604 & 0.047 & 31.370 & 13.766 \\
8  & 17.609 & 0.005 & 35.852 & 18.243 \\
9  & 17.610 & 0.001 & 40.333 & 22.724 \\
10 & 17.610 & 0.000 & 44.815 & 27.205 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{observation}
The block entropy $H_k$ saturates near $17.61$ bits, regardless of scale. The redundancy $R_{10} = 10 \cdot H_1 - H_{10}$ ranges from $22.7$ bits (scale $10^4$) to $27.2$ bits (scale $10^8$), indicating that an i.i.d.\ model over-predicts the information content of 10 consecutive gaps by a factor of approximately~$2.5$.
\end{observation}

The saturation of $H_k$ near $17.6$ bits reflects the effective number of distinct $k$-tuples: at $k = 10$, essentially all 199{,}990 overlapping windows are unique ($n_{\text{distinct}} = 199{,}990$), so $H_{10} \approx \log_2 N$, the maximum entropy of the sample. This means the gap sequence has enough variety that any 10 consecutive gaps form a nearly unique fingerprint, yet the entropies at intermediate $k$ reveal strong short-range structure.

The entropy rate $h_k$ is monotonically decreasing at all scales, consistent with the sub-additivity of entropy. It converges faster at smaller scales (where the gap alphabet is smaller) than at larger scales (where rarer large gaps contribute new information). The convergence of $h_k$ to near zero by $k = 8$--$10$ implies that once you know 7--8 preceding gaps, the next gap adds almost no additional entropy.

\section{Mutual Information}
\label{sec:mi}
% ====================================================================

The mutual information $\MI(g_n; g_{n+\ell})$ captures all statistical dependence between gaps separated by $\ell$ positions, including nonlinear relationships invisible to the Pearson autocorrelation $\rho_\ell$.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Mutual information vs.\ autocorrelation at scale $10^8$.}
\label{tab:mi}
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
\toprule
Lag $\ell$ & MI (bits) & MI corrected & $|\rho_\ell|$ & $\rho_\ell^2 / (2\ln 2)$ \\
\midrule
1   & 0.3224 & 0.3224 & 0.0370 & $9.9 \times 10^{-4}$ \\
2   & 0.0098 & 0.0050 & 0.0133 & $1.3 \times 10^{-4}$ \\
3   & 0.0069 & 0.0020 & 0.0065 & $3.0 \times 10^{-5}$ \\
5   & 0.0076 & 0.0028 & 0.0048 & $1.7 \times 10^{-5}$ \\
10  & 0.0086 & 0.0041 & 0.0033 & $7.7 \times 10^{-6}$ \\
50  & 0.0072 & 0.0025 & 0.0014 & $1.4 \times 10^{-6}$ \\
100 & 0.0072 & 0.0025 & 0.0032 & $7.3 \times 10^{-6}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{observation}[Nonlinear dependence]
\label{obs:nonlinear}
At lag~$1$, $\MI \approx 0.32$ bits while $\rho_1^2 / (2 \ln 2) \approx 10^{-3}$ bits. The mutual information exceeds the Gaussian prediction by a factor of approximately~$300$. This demonstrates that the lag-$1$ dependence is overwhelmingly \emph{nonlinear}: knowing the previous gap tells you $0.32$ bits about the next gap, but only a negligible fraction of this information is carried by the linear correlation.
\end{observation}

The physical explanation is that prime gaps are constrained to be even (for $p > 2$) and their distribution is highly non-Gaussian---it is supported on $\{2, 4, 6, \ldots\}$ with a long right tail. The MI detects the dependence induced by the shared prime $p_{n+1}$ in consecutive gaps $g_n = p_{n+1} - p_n$ and $g_{n+1} = p_{n+2} - p_{n+1}$: this shared endpoint creates a coupling that is fundamentally discrete and nonlinear.

At lags $\ell \ge 2$, the corrected MI is small (0.002--0.005 bits) but consistently positive across all scales, suggesting residual higher-order dependence beyond what 2-dependence models predict.

\begin{observation}
The gap sequence is consistently $6$--$8\%$ more compressible than a random permutation of itself. The ratio $C_{\text{gaps}}/C_{\text{shuffled}}$ increases monotonically from $0.924$ to $0.943$ as the scale grows, consistent with the prediction that prime gaps become ``more random'' (closer to a Poisson process) at larger scales.
\end{observation}

The gap between $C_{\text{gaps}}$ and $C_{\text{shuffled}}$ reflects the sequential structure that shuffling destroys: the short-range dependencies captured by the block entropy analysis. That the ratio approaches~$1$ from below supports the Gallagher conjecture qualitatively: in the limit, the gap sequence should become indistinguishable from an i.i.d.\ process.

\section{Goodness-of-Fit to the Gallagher--Poisson Model}
\label{sec:gof}
% ====================================================================

Gallagher~\cite{Gallagher1976} showed, conditionally on the Hardy--Littlewood $k$-tuple conjecture, that the number of primes in a random interval of length $\lambda \log x$ near $x$ follows a Poisson distribution with mean $\lambda$. An equivalent statement is that the normalized gaps $g_n / \langle g_n \rangle$ converge in distribution to Exp$(1)$.

\subsection{Exponential fit}

Table~\ref{tab:exp-fit} reports the Kolmogorov--Smirnov (KS), Anderson--Darling (AD), and $\chi^2$ test statistics for $g_n / \langle g_n \rangle$ against Exp$(1)$.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Goodness-of-fit statistics for $g_n / \langle g_n \rangle$ against Exp$(1)$.}
\label{tab:exp-fit}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
Scale & KS statistic & AD statistic & $\chi^2$ statistic \\
\midrule
$10^4$ & 0.1597 & 5{,}463 & 147{,}349 \\
$10^5$ & 0.1592 & 5{,}406 & 145{,}143 \\
$10^6$ & 0.1541 & 5{,}007 & 145{,}205 \\
$10^7$ & 0.1466 & 4{,}445 & 107{,}294 \\
$10^8$ & 0.1343 & 3{,}659 & 82{,}103 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

All tests reject overwhelmingly at every scale, which is expected given the sample size ($n \approx 2 \times 10^5$). However, all three test statistics decrease monotonically with scale, confirming that the gap distribution converges toward Exp$(1)$.

\subsection{Poisson counting}

We partition the primes into windows of size $\lambda \log x_{\text{mid}}$ and count primes per window. Table~\ref{tab:poisson} reports the variance-to-mean ratio (the \emph{index of dispersion}).

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering

\label{tab:poisson}
\begin{tabular}{cc}
\toprule
Scale & $\sigma^2 / \mu$ \\
\midrule
$10^4$ & 0.722 \\
$10^5$ & 0.723 \\
$10^6$ & 0.733 \\
$10^7$ & 0.746 \\
$10^8$ & 0.768 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{observation}[Underdispersion]
Prime counts in short intervals are systematically \emph{underdispersed} relative to Poisson: $\sigma^2/\mu < 1$ at all scales. However, the ratio increases monotonically toward~$1$, consistent with convergence to the Gallagher prediction. The underdispersion reflects the ``repulsion'' between consecutive primes induced by the sieve: a prime at $p$ eliminates candidates near $p$, reducing variance below the Poisson level. This effect weakens as the window size grows relative to typical gap sizes.
\end{observation}

\section{Conditional Entropy by Residue Class}
\label{sec:conditional}
% ====================================================================

All primes $p > 3$ satisfy $p \equiv 1$ or $5 \pmod{6}$. We partition primes by residue class and measure the gap entropy within each class.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Single-gap entropy $H_1$ (bits) by prime residue class mod~$6$.}
\label{tab:cond-entropy}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
Scale & $H_1$ (all) & $H_1$ ($p \equiv 1$) & $H_1$ ($p \equiv 5$) \\
\midrule
$10^4$ & 4.035 & 6.882 & 6.924 \\
$10^5$ & 4.053 & 6.921 & 6.964 \\
$10^6$ & 4.128 & 7.073 & 7.122 \\
$10^7$ & 4.289 & 7.393 & 7.468 \\
$10^8$ & 4.481 & 7.799 & 7.860 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{observation}
Primes $\equiv 5 \pmod{6}$ have consistently higher gap entropy ($\approx 0.05$ bits more) than primes $\equiv 1 \pmod{6}$ across all scales. This asymmetry connects to the Hardy--Littlewood singular series: the conjecture predicts that gaps divisible by~$3$ (which contribute to specific residue patterns) are enhanced by a factor $\mathfrak{S}(6)/\mathfrak{S}(4) = 2$, creating slightly different gap distributions for the two residue classes.
\end{observation}

\begin{remark}
The class-restricted entropies ($\approx 7$--$8$ bits) are much larger than the full-sequence entropy ($\approx 4$--$4.5$ bits). This is because class-restricted ``gaps'' are computed between consecutive primes in the same class, which are on average twice as large and span a wider range of values, yielding more uncertainty per gap.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ====================================================================

\subsection{The nonlinear dependence gap}

The most striking finding is the enormous discrepancy between mutual information and autocorrelation at lag~$1$ (Observation~\ref{obs:nonlinear}). The autocorrelation $\rho_1 \approx -0.04$ has been noted previously~\cite{LemkeOliver2016} and attributed to the shared prime $p_{n+1}$. Our MI analysis reveals that this shared prime creates a dependence $300$ times stronger than the linear correlation suggests. This has implications for any probabilistic model of prime gaps: models calibrated on autocorrelation alone (as in~\cite{Gallagher1976, Chase2020}) miss the dominant component of the nearest-neighbor dependence.

\subsection{Convergence to the Gallagher prediction}

All five experiments independently confirm that the gap sequence becomes ``more random'' at larger scales:
\begin{itemize}
\item Entropy rate $h_k$ at fixed $k$ increases with scale (more unpredictability per gap).
\item MI at fixed lag decreases with scale (less dependence).
\item LZ complexity ratio approaches~$1$ (less compressibility).
\item KS and AD statistics decrease (better exponential fit).
\item Variance-to-mean ratio increases toward~$1$ (less underdispersion).
\end{itemize}

These five measures provide independent quantifications of the \emph{rate} at which the Gallagher prediction takes hold. The KS statistic decreases roughly as $0.16 - 0.003 \log_{10}(x)$, suggesting full convergence is very slow.
